# Urban Green Space and Self-Reported Well-being

**Status**: Working paper, version 3 (2026-05). Not yet peer-reviewed.

**Authors**: A. Researcher (Example University), B. Colleague
(Example Institute)

## Abstract (fictional)

Access to urban green space is increasingly discussed as a component
of healthy city design, but longitudinal evidence remains limited. We
use three waves (2018, 2021, 2024) of a city panel survey
(N = 4,200 adults in 12 districts) to examine the relationship between
residential green-space access (within 500 m, from land-use data) and
self-reported well-being (WHO-5 index). In individual fixed-effects
models, an increase in green-space access was associated with higher
self-reported well-being (b = 0.14 SD, 95% CI 0.05–0.23). The
association was larger for respondents over 60. Because access is not
randomly assigned and well-being is self-reported, estimates should
not be interpreted as causal effects. Residential sorting and
unmeasured time-varying confounders remain plausible alternative
explanations.
